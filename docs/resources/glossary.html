Warehouse Storage Glossary

Learn the terminology of pallet racking and warehouse storage. From rack components to industry terms, here's your reference guide.

Jump to section:

Racking Types

Selective Rack (Selective Pallet Rack)

The most common type of pallet racking system. Provides direct access to every pallet position from the aisle. Ideal for operations with diverse SKUs that require individual pallet accessibility. Available in roll-formed (bolted) and structural (welded) configurations.

Related: Heavy Duty Pallet Racking

Drive-In Rack

A high-density storage system where forklifts enter the racking structure to place pallets on rails multiple positions deep. Best for high-volume, low-SKU inventory where LIFO (Last In, First Out) inventory rotation is acceptable. Can increase storage density by 75% compared to selective rack.

Drive-Through Rack

Similar to drive-in rack but accessible from both ends, allowing FIFO (First In, First Out) inventory flow. Forklifts can enter from either side of the rack structure. Less dense than drive-in but provides better inventory rotation.

Push-Back Rack

A dynamic storage system using nested carts on inclined rails. Pallets are loaded from the front aisle; each new pallet pushes the previous ones back. When retrieved, remaining pallets roll forward. Provides high density with LIFO rotation while maintaining aisle accessibility.

Pallet Flow Rack (Gravity Flow Rack)

A dynamic system where pallets are loaded at the back and flow forward on roller conveyors or wheels. Provides automatic FIFO rotation. Often used for date-sensitive inventory like food and beverage products.

Cantilever Rack

A racking system with horizontal arms extending from vertical columns, providing an open front without vertical obstructions. Designed for long, bulky, or irregularly shaped items like lumber, pipes, furniture, and sheet goods.

Carton Flow Rack

Similar to pallet flow but designed for cartons and cases rather than pallets. Uses tilted shelves with rollers or wheels to move products forward. Common in pick/pack operations for order fulfillment.

Double-Deep Rack

Selective racking stored two pallets deep, accessed with a reach truck or double-deep forklift. Provides about 40% more storage than standard selective while maintaining reasonable accessibility. Second pallet position is blocked by the front pallet.

Structural Rack

Pallet racking constructed from hot-rolled structural steel shapes (C-channels, I-beams) with bolted connections. Heavier duty than roll-formed rack. Used in high-impact environments, cold storage, or where maximum durability is required.

Roll-Formed Rack

Pallet racking constructed from cold-rolled steel formed into specific profiles. Lighter and less expensive than structural rack but still rated for substantial loads. The most common type of selective rack in warehouses.

Narrow Aisle (NA) Rack

Racking systems designed for aisles of 8-10 feet, accessed by turret trucks or swing-reach trucks. Increases storage density compared to standard wide-aisle configurations while maintaining selectivity.

Very Narrow Aisle (VNA) Rack

Racking systems with aisles as narrow as 5-6 feet, requiring specialized man-up or wire-guided turret trucks. Maximizes storage density in selective configurations. Often includes guide rails or wire guidance systems.

Rack Components

Upright Frame (Upright)

The vertical component of pallet racking consisting of two columns connected by horizontal and diagonal bracing. Uprights provide the structural foundation and are typically anchored to the floor. Specified by height and depth.

Column

The vertical posts that make up an upright frame. Columns feature a pattern of holes or slots (the "teardrop" or other profile) that accept beam connectors at various heights.

Beam (Load Beam, Step Beam, Box Beam)

The horizontal load-bearing members that connect upright frames and support pallets or decking. Step beams have a ledge for wire decking support. Box beams are fully enclosed rectangles. Specified by length and capacity.

Beam Connector (Beam End Connector)

The hardware at each end of a beam that connects to the upright column. Various profiles exist (teardrop, keystone, structural clip) that must match the upright's hole pattern. Includes safety clips or locks to prevent accidental dislodging.

Wire Deck (Wire Decking)

Welded wire mesh panels that sit on beams to provide a solid support surface for pallets. Allows light penetration, sprinkler water flow, and visual inspection. Specified by dimensions and capacity.

Pallet Support (Pallet Support Bar)

A crossbar that spans between front and back beams to provide additional pallet support, especially for undersized pallets or those with compromised bottom boards.

Row Spacer

A horizontal member that connects back-to-back upright frames, maintaining proper spacing and adding structural rigidity to the rack system.

Shim (Shim Plate)

Thin metal plates placed under upright baseplates to level the rack on uneven floors.

Baseplate (Footplate)

The steel plate at the bottom of each column that distributes load to the floor and provides anchor bolt holes for securing the rack.

Anchor Bolt (Floor Anchor)

Fasteners that secure rack baseplates to the concrete floor. Required by building codes in most jurisdictions. Various types include wedge anchors, drop-in anchors, and epoxy anchors.

Column Protector (Post Protector)

A heavy-gauge steel guard installed around the bottom of rack columns to protect against forklift impact damage. Available in bolt-on or wrap-around styles.

End-of-Aisle Guard (Row End Protector)

A structural steel guard installed at the end of rack rows to protect against forklift collisions from cross aisles.

Frame Protector (Corner Guard)

Protective devices installed on rack frames at floor level to absorb or deflect forklift impacts.

Safety Clip (Safety Pin, Beam Lock)

A locking device that secures beam connectors to upright columns, preventing beams from being accidentally dislodged. Required by code in most applications.

Backstop (Pallet Stop)

A bar or angle installed at the back of rack beams to prevent pallets from being pushed off the back, especially in back-to-back configurations.

Guide Rail

Steel rails installed on the floor along the edge of rack aisles to guide forklifts and prevent them from hitting the rack structure. Common in narrow aisle and very narrow aisle applications.

Crossbar (Cross Brace)

Horizontal bracing within an upright frame that connects the two columns for structural stability.

Diagonal Brace

Diagonal bracing within an upright frame that provides structural rigidity and load distribution between columns.

Measurements & Specifications

Beam Capacity

The maximum uniformly distributed load (UDL) a pair of beams can safely support, typically expressed in pounds per pair. Capacity varies by beam length, profile, and connection type. Always specified with a safety factor included.

Frame Capacity

The maximum load an upright frame can support, determined by column gauge, frame depth, and height. Expressed in pounds per column or per frame.

Clear Height

The vertical distance from the floor to the lowest overhead obstruction (typically roof trusses, sprinkler pipes, or lighting). Critical for determining maximum rack height.

Upright Height

The overall height of an upright frame from baseplate to top. Selected based on clear height minus required clearances for fire sprinklers and lighting.

Upright Depth (Frame Depth)

The front-to-back dimension of an upright frame. Standard depths range from 36" to 48" for most pallet rack applications. Depth is selected based on pallet depth plus required clearances.

Beam Length (Beam Span)

The face-to-face measurement between uprights that a beam spans. Common lengths include 96", 108", and 144" for storing 2 or 3 pallets per bay.

Bay

The space between two adjacent upright frames. A "bay" typically holds 2 or 3 pallets side by side depending on beam length and pallet width.

Level

A horizontal storage position within a rack bay. A rack system with 4 levels of storage has 4 beam pairs where pallets can be stored.

Pallet Position

A single storage location for one pallet. Used as the standard unit for describing warehouse capacity (e.g., "5,000 pallet positions").

Aisle Width

The clear distance between facing rack rows. Standard wide aisles are 10-13 feet for sit-down forklifts, 8-10 feet for reach trucks, and 5-6 feet for VNA equipment.

Flue Space

The clear space between pallets or rack components required by fire codes for fire suppression effectiveness. Transverse flue is the side-to-side space between pallets; longitudinal flue is front-to-back space in back-to-back configurations.

Top of Steel (TOS)

The elevation of the top beam level in a rack system. Measured from floor to the top surface of the highest beam. Must maintain required clearance to sprinklers.

Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL)

A load that is evenly spread across the entire beam span. Beam capacities are typically rated for UDL conditions. Point loads or uneven loading reduce effective capacity.

Operations & Inventory

FIFO (First In, First Out)

An inventory rotation method where the oldest inventory (first in) is picked and shipped first. Essential for perishable goods, date-coded products, and items with expiration dates. Achieved with selective rack, drive-through, or flow rack systems.

LIFO (Last In, First Out)

An inventory rotation method where the newest inventory (last in) is picked first. Acceptable for non-perishable goods with no expiration concerns. Drive-in and push-back rack systems operate on LIFO principles.

SKU (Stock Keeping Unit)

A unique identifier for each distinct product in inventory. The number of SKUs in a warehouse affects rack type selectionhigh-SKU operations typically require more selective access.

Cube Utilization

The percentage of available warehouse cubic feet actually used for storage. High-density rack systems increase cube utilization compared to floor stacking or selective-only configurations.

Slotting

The process of determining optimal storage locations for products based on pick frequency, size, weight, and other factors. Proper slotting improves picking efficiency and reduces travel time.

Pick Face

The location from which products are picked for orders. In pallet rack, typically the lowest level accessible without lifting equipment. In case pick operations, mid-level positions may serve as pick faces.

Bulk Storage

High-volume storage of palletized goods, typically in upper rack levels. Replenishes pick face locations as inventory is depleted.

Cross-Docking

A logistics practice where incoming goods are transferred directly to outbound shipping with minimal storage time. Reduces need for deep storage but may require staging areas.

Throughput

The rate at which goods move through a warehouse, typically measured in pallets per hour or cases per hour. Rack type and layout significantly impact achievable throughput.

Turn Rate (Inventory Turns)

The number of times inventory is completely sold and replaced in a given period (usually annually). High-turn operations may prioritize accessibility over density; low-turn operations may benefit from high-density storage.

Safety & Compliance

RMI (Rack Manufacturers Institute)

The industry trade association that publishes specifications and guidelines for the design, testing, and use of industrial steel storage racks. RMI standards (ANSI MH16.1) are the basis for most rack engineering and permitting.

ANSI MH16.1

The American National Standards Institute standard for the design, testing, and utilization of industrial steel storage racks. Developed by RMI and referenced by building codes nationwide. Covers load application, seismic design, and safety requirements.

Seismic Design Category (SDC)

A classification from A through F that defines seismic design requirements for structures based on location and building use. Higher categories require more robust rack connections, bracing, and engineering documentation.

PE-Stamped Drawings (Engineered Drawings)

Rack layout and structural drawings reviewed, approved, and stamped by a licensed Professional Engineer. Required for permits in many jurisdictions, especially in seismic zones.

Load Capacity Plaque (Capacity Placard)

A sign posted on rack systems showing the rated load capacity per level, configuration, and any special loading requirements. Required by OSHA and most building codes.

Rack Safety Inspection

A systematic examination of rack systems to identify damage, improper loading, missing components, or other safety hazards. ANSI MH16.1 recommends regular inspections by trained personnel.

Related: Rack Safety Inspections

Damage Assessment

The process of evaluating rack components after a forklift impact or other incident to determine if repair, replacement, or immediate unloading is required.

In-Rack Sprinkler

Fire suppression sprinkler heads installed within the rack structure at intermediate levels, rather than only at ceiling level. Often required for high-piled storage or certain commodity classifications.

High-Piled Storage

Storage of combustible materials above specific height thresholds (typically 12 feet), triggering additional fire code requirements including sprinkler upgrades, permits, and clearances.

Commodity Classification

Fire code classification of stored goods based on combustibility and fire behavior. Classes range from Class I (non-combustible) through Class IV (free-burning combustibles), plus special high-hazard categories.

Industry Terms

3PL (Third-Party Logistics)

Companies that provide outsourced logistics and warehousing services. 3PL facilities often require flexible, reconfigurable rack systems to accommodate multiple clients with varying needs.

Related: 3PL & Logistics Solutions

WMS (Warehouse Management System)

Software that controls and manages warehouse operations including receiving, putaway, picking, packing, and shipping. Rack layouts must be compatible with WMS location naming conventions.

GMA Pallet

The standard pallet size used in grocery and consumer goods industries, measuring 48" x 40". Named for the Grocery Manufacturers Association. Rack beam lengths are often designed around GMA pallet dimensions.

Stringer Pallet

A pallet constructed with solid boards (stringers) running lengthwise between the top and bottom deck boards. Can only be lifted from two sides unless notched.

Block Pallet

A pallet constructed with solid blocks at the corners and center, allowing four-way forklift access. Stronger than stringer pallets but more expensive.

Reach Truck

A narrow-aisle forklift with a mast that extends forward to place or retrieve pallets. Designed for aisles as narrow as 8-10 feet. More maneuverable than sit-down forklifts.

Order Picker (Stock Picker)

A lift truck where the operator rises with the forks to manually pick items from elevated rack locations. Common in case-pick and piece-pick operations.

Turret Truck (Swing-Reach Truck)

A very narrow aisle forklift with forks that swing 90 degrees to either side, allowing it to pick from both sides of the aisle without turning. Used in VNA applications.

Man-Up Truck

A narrow aisle lift truck where the operator compartment rises with the forks, providing visual confirmation of pallet placement at height.

Wire Guidance

A navigation system using wires embedded in warehouse floors to guide VNA forklifts precisely down aisles. Prevents rack damage and allows higher travel speeds.

Teardrop

The most common beam-to-column connection style, featuring teardrop-shaped holes in the column that accept corresponding connector tabs. Allows tool-free beam adjustment.

Cold Storage

Temperature-controlled warehouse environments, including coolers (32-55F) and freezers (below 32F, often -10F to -20F). Requires corrosion-resistant rack finishes and specialized installation practices.
