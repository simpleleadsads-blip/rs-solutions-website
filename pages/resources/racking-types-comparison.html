Pallet Racking Types Compared

Different rack configurations serve different purposes. Understanding the trade-offs helps you choose the right system for your operation.

The main pallet rack types are selective (direct access to every pallet), drive-in (high density, LIFO), push-back (high density with aisle access), pallet flow (automatic FIFO), and cantilever (for long/bulky items). Most warehouses use selective rack as their primary system, with high-density options in specific zones. The right choice depends on your SKU count, inventory turn rate, and space constraints.

Selective Pallet Rack

The most common rack type—and for good reason.

Selective rack provides direct access to every pallet position from the aisle. It's the default choice for most warehouse applications and the most versatile system available.

Advantages

  • Access any pallet without moving others
  • Works with any forklift type
  • Easy to reconfigure beam heights
  • Lowest cost per pallet position
  • Supports FIFO with proper warehouse management

Limitations

  • Requires more aisle space than high-density options
  • Lower storage density (typically 40-50% floor space is aisles)

Best For

High-SKU operations, 3PL facilities, general distribution, any operation requiring individual pallet access.

Drive-In Rack

Maximum density for bulk storage.

Forklifts drive into the rack structure to place pallets on rails multiple positions deep. Eliminates aisles between rows, dramatically increasing storage capacity.

Advantages

  • Up to 75% more storage vs. selective
  • Ideal for homogeneous inventory
  • Lower cost per pallet than other high-density options
  • Great for cold storage (expensive space)

Limitations

  • LIFO only—last pallet in is first out
  • Only one SKU per lane
  • Slower put/pull than selective
  • Higher potential for forklift damage
  • Not suitable for high-SKU operations

Best For

Low-SKU, high-volume storage. Seasonal inventory staging. Cold storage where space costs are high. Products where LIFO is acceptable.

Push-Back Rack

High density with aisle-side access.

Pallets sit on nested carts that roll on inclined rails. Each new pallet pushes the previous ones back. When you retrieve, remaining pallets roll forward automatically.

Advantages

  • High density (2-6 pallets deep)
  • Forklift never enters the rack structure
  • Faster than drive-in for put/pull
  • Less damage risk than drive-in
  • Different SKUs can occupy each lane

Limitations

  • LIFO inventory rotation
  • Higher cost than selective or drive-in
  • Cart mechanisms require maintenance
  • Limited to about 6 pallets deep

Best For

Operations needing density plus faster throughput than drive-in. Multiple SKUs with moderate depth. Buffer stock and production staging.

Pallet Flow Rack

Automatic FIFO rotation built in.

Pallets are loaded at the back and flow forward on gravity roller conveyors. Pick from the front, and the next pallet rolls into position automatically.

Advantages

  • True FIFO—essential for date-sensitive products
  • Very deep storage (up to 20 pallets)
  • Separate load and pick aisles
  • Automatic product presentation
  • Excellent throughput for high-volume SKUs

Limitations

  • Highest cost per pallet position
  • Requires consistent pallet quality
  • Roller/wheel maintenance required
  • Not economical for slow-moving SKUs

Best For

Date-coded inventory (food, beverage, pharmaceuticals). High-volume, consistent SKUs. Operations where FIFO is mandatory.

Cantilever Rack

For long, bulky, and odd-shaped items.

Horizontal arms extend from vertical columns, providing an open front with no vertical obstructions. Designed for items that don't fit standard pallet dimensions.

Advantages

  • No front columns blocking load/unload
  • Handles any length—lumber, pipes, sheet goods
  • 100% selectivity for stored items
  • Adjustable arm heights
  • Works with forklifts, side-loaders, cranes

Limitations

  • Only practical for long/bulky items
  • Requires adequate aisle width for load lengths
  • Not as space-efficient as pallet rack for standard goods

Best For

Lumber yards, pipe storage, steel service centers, furniture, carpet rolls, sheet metal, building materials, any long or irregularly shaped inventory.

How to Choose

Start with SKU count

High SKU variety? Selective rack should be your primary system. Low SKU count with deep inventory? High-density systems make more sense.

Consider inventory rotation

Need strict FIFO? Pallet flow is the only system that guarantees it automatically. LIFO acceptable? Drive-in or push-back can provide major density gains.

Evaluate space costs

Expensive space? (Cold storage, high-rent markets) Higher-density systems pay back faster. Space isn't the constraint? Selective rack's flexibility may be worth more than density.

Think about throughput

High velocity, lots of activity? Selective provides fastest put/pull. Bulk staging with occasional access? Drive-in works well. Need density AND speed? Push-back is the compromise.

Most facilities use multiple types

A hybrid approach often makes the most sense: selective for active pick faces and diverse inventory, high-density for bulk reserve or specific product lines. We design integrated systems using the right rack type for each zone.
